When Multimodal AI Truly Helps in Healthcare: A Benchmark on EHR and Chest X-Ray Fusion

March 3, 2026 • 10 min read • By Dr. Bradley J. Erickson

## When Multimodal AI Truly Helps in Healthcare: A Benchmark on EHR and Chest X-Ray Fusion

The future of clinical decision support hinges on artificial intelligence, promising to revolutionize how we diagnose, treat, and monitor patients. Machine learning models are already showing immense potential in forecasting disease onset, stratifying patient risk, and even personalizing treatment pathways. However, a significant frontier remains: integrating the vast, heterogeneous tapestry of patient data – from detailed Electronic Health Records (EHR) to critical medical images like Chest X-rays (CXR). This integration, known as multimodal learning, holds the key to creating a truly holistic patient view, but its real-world utility, especially under challenging conditions like missing data and the imperative for fairness, has remained largely unexplored. This groundbreaking paper introduces **CareBench**, a comprehensive benchmark that sheds light on exactly when, how, and why multimodal learning succeeds or falters in healthcare, providing crucial guidance for the development of clinically deployable AI systems.

### The Challenge of Clinical Data Integration

Imagine a physician trying to piece together a patient's health story. They consult the patient's EHR, a rich digital chronicle of their medical history, diagnoses, medications, lab results, and vital signs, often spanning years. Then, they examine a recent CXR, a snapshot offering crucial visual insights into the patient's cardiopulmonary status. Each data source tells a part of the story, but the true diagnostic power often lies in their combined interpretation. This is the essence of multimodal learning in healthcare: leveraging diverse data types to build a more complete and accurate understanding of a patient's condition.

While the promise is clear, the practical application of multimodal AI faces substantial hurdles. Clinical data is inherently messy and incomplete. Patients might not have a recent CXR for every ICU stay, or certain lab values might be missing. Moreover, different data types, like the continuous, temporal stream of EHR data versus the static image of a CXR, present unique computational challenges for integration. Beyond technical performance, there's a critical ethical dimension: ensuring that these powerful AI models do not perpetuate or even amplify existing health disparities across different demographic groups. Current benchmarks in clinical machine learning often focus on single data types or assume perfectly complete data, offering limited insight into these real-world complexities.

This is precisely the problem CareBench sets out to address. The researchers recognized a critical gap in systematic understanding: _when does multimodal fusion truly improve clinical prediction?_ How do different strategies for combining data compare? How robust are these models when faced with realistic scenarios of missing data? And, crucially, do multimodal models achieve algorithmic fairness across diverse patient subgroups?

### CareBench: A Systematic Approach to Multimodal Evaluation

To answer these fundamental questions, the team developed CareBench, a rigorous framework for evaluating multimodal fusion of EHR and CXR data. Their work is built upon three core components:

   

• **Standardized Data Pipeline:** An open-source and reproducible pipeline to extract and process data from two of the largest real-world ICU databases, MIMIC-IV (Electronic Health Records) and MIMIC-CXR (Chest X-rays). This ensures consistent data cohorts for fair comparison.

   

• **Unified Toolkit of Models:** A comprehensive, open-source toolkit that implements a wide array of machine learning models, ranging from basic unimodal baselines (using only EHR or only CXR) to advanced, state-of-the-art fusion architectures. This allows for direct comparison of different fusion strategies.

   

• **Rigorous Evaluation Protocol:** A sophisticated evaluation approach that goes beyond simply measuring predictive accuracy. It systematically assesses model behavior under varying degrees of modality missingness and analyzes algorithmic fairness across different patient subgroups, providing a principled investigation into robustness and equity.

By establishing this robust benchmark, CareBench aims to provide actionable insights for researchers and developers, guiding them toward building clinically deployable multimodal AI systems that are not only effective but also reliable and fair.

## What They Did: Building the CareBench Framework

### Data Acquisition and Cohort Construction

The foundation of CareBench lies in its carefully constructed datasets, drawn from two large-scale, real-world ICU databases: **MIMIC-IV** and **MIMIC-CXR**. MIMIC-IV contains de-identified electronic health records for adult patients admitted to intensive care units (ICUs) or emergency departments at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (BIDMC) between 2008 and 2019. MIMIC-CXR is a publicly available dataset of chest radiographs from the same institution, allowing a subset of patients to be matched between the two datasets.

The researchers constructed two primary cohorts of ICU stays:

   

•

        **The Base Cohort:** This cohort started with 73,181 ICU stays from MIMIC-IV. A rigorous exclusion process was applied to ensure clinical and temporal consistency, removing stays with missing essential documentation, implausible temporal records, very short ICU stays (less than 6 hours or less than 48 hours for robust prediction), or non-urgent admissions. This refined the base cohort to **26,947 ICU stays**, representing clinically meaningful acute episodes.

   

   

•

        **The Matched Subset:** To enable multimodal evaluation, this subset was further restricted to patients from the base cohort who also had at least one chest radiograph available within a specific temporal window (24 hours before to 48 hours after ICU admission). This yielded a matched subset of **7,149 ICU stays**, where both structured EHR data and CXR images were available. This "complete-modality" subset was crucial for understanding the maximum potential of multimodal fusion.

   

The exclusion criteria were meticulously detailed, ensuring data quality and clinical relevance. For instance, ICU stays shorter than 48 hours were excluded from the base cohort to ensure sufficient longitudinal information for robust prediction, a common challenge in real-world clinical data.

### Feature Extraction and Preprocessing

For each ICU stay, a comprehensive set of structured EHR features was extracted from MIMIC-IV. This included data across multiple physiological domains: vital signs (heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, SpO2, temperature, glucose), neurological status (Glasgow Coma Scale), cardiac rhythm, respiratory support parameters (O2 flow, FiO2), fluid balance (urine output), and body weight. Features with a missingness rate greater than 90% were empirically excluded, and treatment-related variables (e.g., CRRT, invasive line placement) were removed to prevent label leakage.

EHR data, being temporal, was resampled to an hourly resolution. Missing values were imputed using forward filling and median imputation, with binary mask columns retained to indicate data availability (as missingness itself can be clinically informative). Robust normalization was applied to continuous variables to mitigate outlier influence. For CXR data, only frontal-view images with an Anterior-Posterior (AP) projection were included, and the most recent CXR prior to the prediction timepoint was selected to reflect the patient's latest cardiopulmonary status, ensuring temporal and clinical alignment with EHR data.

### Benchmark Models and Tasks

CareBench benchmarked a broad range of models, categorized into three groups:

   

•

        **Uni-modal Baselines:** These models establish a reference performance for each modality individually. For EHR, classic Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Transformer models (known for capturing temporal dependencies) were used. For CXR, a ResNet-50 model (pre-trained on ImageNet) was employed.

   

   

•

        **Complete-Modality Multimodal Fusion Methods:** This group assumes all modalities are present during both training and inference. It includes simple strategies like _Late Fusion_ (concatenating unimodal embeddings) and more advanced architectures such as _UTDE_, _DAFT_, _MMTM_, _AUG_, and _InfoReg_, which employ sophisticated mechanisms for cross-modal interaction and information exchange.

   

   

•

        **Missing-Modality Multimodal Fusion Methods:** Crucially, this group includes models explicitly designed to handle incomplete inputs, addressing the pervasive issue of missing data in clinical practice. This includes methods like _HEALNet_, _Flex-MoE_, _DrFuse_, _UMSE_, _ShaSpec_, _M3Care_, _MedFuse_, and _SMIL_. These models span diverse design paradigms, from disentangled representations to adaptive fusion strategies.

   

Models were evaluated on three clinically relevant downstream tasks:

   

•

        **Phenotyping Classification:** Predicting the set of acute and chronic conditions present during an ICU stay (25 phenotypes derived from ICD-9 and ICD-10 codes). This is a multi-label classification task, assessed by AUROC, AUPRC, F1 score, precision, recall, specificity, and accuracy.

   

   

•

        **Mortality Prediction:** Predicting in-hospital mortality within the first 48 hours of ICU admission, formulated as a binary classification problem. Evaluated using AUROC, AUPRC, F1-score, accuracy, precision, recall, and specificity.

   

   

•

        **Length-of-Stay (LoS) Prediction:** Accurately estimating the remaining hospital stay (RLOS) using the first 48 hours of data. This is a multi-class classification problem with ordinal structure, evaluated using ACC, F1 score, and Cohen's Kappa weighted quadratic.

   

The evaluation protocol was rigorous, using patient-level train/validation/test splits and consistent training settings across tasks. Bayesian hyperparameter optimization was performed to ensure fair comparison and optimal model configurations.

## Results: Key Findings from CareBench

### RQ1: When does multimodal fusion benefit medical tasks?

The study first evaluated all models on the _matched subset_ (where both EHR and CXR data were complete) to understand the maximum potential of multimodal fusion.

    Key Finding 1

   

**Multimodal fusion generally improves performance under complete-modality settings.** When all modalities are available, multimodal models consistently achieve the best performance across all tasks and evaluation metrics. For phenotyping, leading fusion approaches like InfoReg (**AUPRC 0.495**) and AUG (**AUROC 0.742**) significantly outperformed the strongest unimodal EHR Transformer (AUPRC 0.472, AUROC 0.724). For mortality prediction, DrFuse achieved the strongest AUPRC (**0.481**) compared to LSTM (0.451), and MedFuse attained the highest AUROC (**0.851** vs. 0.821 from Transformer). LoS prediction showed more modest gains, with ShaSpec achieving the highest Kappa (**0.198**) relative to the best unimodal model (LSTM, 0.192).

These results clearly demonstrate that CXR provides valuable complementary information to structured EHR data, leading to richer patient representations and improved predictive performance when both are present. However, the gains for mortality and LoS were often smaller and less significant than for phenotyping. The researchers suggest this is because EHR's dense physiological signals and lab measurements already explain much of these endpoints, while CXR provides a more indirect and condition-dependent signal.

    Key Finding 2

   

**Multimodal benefits concentrate in modality-distributed phenotypes.** Phenotyping showed the largest and most stable gains. Diseases like congestive heart failure (CHF), coronary heart disease (CorAth/HD), COPD, and liver disease benefited most from multimodal learning. These conditions are characterized by "modality-distributed phenotypes," where CXR captures structural or pulmonary manifestations, while EHR encodes longitudinal risk factors and comorbidity history. Multimodal models successfully leverage these complementary signals. In contrast, conditions like sepsis, primarily driven by acute physiological changes, saw limited fusion benefits as CXR offered only indirect cues.

This distinction highlights a crucial clinical insight: multimodal AI is most effective when modalities provide truly complementary information that cannot be fully captured by a single source alone.

### RQ2: How do different fusion strategies compare under complete-modality settings?

The study compared 14 fusion methods, from simple concatenation to sophisticated cross-modal attention and representation disentanglement.

    Key Finding 3

   

**Cross-modal learning mechanisms capture clinically meaningful dependencies that concatenation misses.** Methods enabling cross-modal learning substantially outperformed naive Late Fusion, which simply concatenates unimodal embeddings. For phenotyping, InfoReg (**0.495 AUPRC**), AUG (**0.493 AUPRC**), DrFuse (**0.493 AUPRC**), and UTDE (**0.492 AUPRC**) all surpassed Late Fusion (0.489 AUPRC). This performance gap reflects the clinical reality that imaging findings need to be interpreted in context of physiological state. For instance, in pneumonia (PNA), advanced methods like DrFuse (**0.500 AUPRC**) and UTDE (**0.495 AUPRC**) showed further gains over Late Fusion (0.493 AUPRC), reflecting how they capture the conditional dependencies between CXR findings and EHR symptoms.

This finding underscores that sophisticated fusion strategies are not just about combining data, but about enabling the AI to "reason" across modalities in a clinically intelligent way, much like a physician would. Simple concatenation, which treats modalities as independent, misses these crucial interactions.

    Key Finding 4

   

**EHR's longitudinal richness creates modality imbalance that architectural complexity alone cannot overcome.** Methods explicitly addressing modality imbalance, such as **AUG** (rank 4.8) and **InfoReg** (rank 4.6), consistently achieved superior performance. This reflects a fundamental characteristic of clinical data: EHR offers rich, longitudinal signals (hourly vital signs, lab trends) capturing disease progression in granular detail. CXR, on the other hand, provides a static snapshot at one timepoint. This creates an inherent information asymmetry where the EHR modality can dominate the learning process. Models like InfoReg (which slows learning from information-rich modalities) and AUG (which iteratively boosts weaker modalities) succeed by counteracting this imbalance, demonstrating that addressing data density differences is more critical than just complex architectures.

This insight is pivotal: effective multimodal fusion in healthcare must account for the inherent differences in information density between continuous monitoring data (EHR) and intermittent imaging studies (CXR).

### RQ3: How robust are multimodal fusion methods to modality missingness?

Modality missingness is a pervasive issue in clinical practice, with approximately 75% of ICU stays in the base cohort lacking paired CXRs. The study evaluated models on the _base cohort_ (realistic missingness) and conducted controlled experiments varying CXR missingness from 0% to 80%.

    Key Finding 5

   

**Specialized architectural and algorithmic designs are essential for leveraging incomplete multimodal data.** In the base cohort, the EHR-only Transformer (mortality F1-score **0.679**, LoS Kappa **0.204**) often outperformed multimodal models designed for complete data (e.g., MMTM: mortality F1 0.663, LoS Kappa 0.1714). This highlights that naively applying complete-case models in real-world scenarios often fails. However, models specifically designed to handle missing modalities, such as **MedFuse** (highest AUROC **0.874** for mortality, best Kappa **0.213** and F1-score **0.203** for LoS), **M3Care**, and **HEALNet**, outperformed both the unimodal baseline and complete-case fusion methods.

This finding is a strong call to action: for multimodal AI to be clinically useful, it must be intrinsically robust to missing data. Models that explicitly account for and adapt to modality absence are far superior to those that assume perfect data completeness.

    Key Finding 6

   

**Severe missingness amplifies modality imbalance, hindering multimodal fusion in realistic settings.** In the base cohort, EHR was present for all 26,947 ICU stays, while CXR was available for only 7,149 cases (~25%). This distributional imbalance, combined with EHR's longitudinal richness versus CXR's single timepoint, created an amplified dominance effect during training. Most multimodal fusion methods, implicitly assuming balanced modality contributions, optimized primarily on EHR-driven gradients. However, models like **InfoReg** and **AUG**, which explicitly counteract modality imbalance in their learning algorithms, significantly outperformed other methods in the base cohort. Controlled missingness experiments further showed InfoReg and AUG degrading notably slower as missing ratios increased from 0% to 80%.

This reveals a critical interaction: modality missingness exacerbates the inherent information asymmetry between EHR and CXR. Models that can balance learning across modalities, even when one is scarce, are crucial for graceful degradation and robust performance in real-world clinical settings.

### RQ4: Do multimodal fusion methods achieve algorithmic fairness?

Beyond predictive performance, the study investigated algorithmic fairness by stratifying model performance across racial subgroups, using metrics like AUPRC gap, TPR gap, FPR gap, and ECE gap.

    Key Finding 7

   

**Multimodal fusion does not inherently improve algorithmic fairness.** Despite improving predictive performance, multimodal fusion did not consistently reduce subgroup disparities. All multimodal models exhibited larger AUPRC gaps than the unimodal Transformer baseline, indicating increased performance disparity across race subgroups. Several high-performing multimodal methods, including DrFuse, ShaSpec, SMIL, and InfoReg, consistently demonstrated higher gaps across AUPRC, TPR, FPR, and calibration (ECE) compared to the unimodal Transformer. This suggests that performance gains from multimodal fusion do not automatically translate into improved fairness, and may even exacerbate disparities.

This is a sobering but vital finding. Simply combining more data, even if it improves overall accuracy, does not guarantee equitable outcomes across patient groups. In some cases, it may even worsen existing biases, highlighting the need for explicit fairness considerations in multimodal AI development.

    Key Finding 8

   

**Fairness violations are driven more by unequal sensitivity than false positives.** Across nearly all models, TPR gaps (unequal sensitivity) were consistently larger than FPR gaps (unequal false positive rates). This pattern indicates that subgroup disparities primarily manifest as _under-detection_ of certain demographic groups (i.e., higher false negatives), rather than over-detection. This has important implications for clinical deployment, as under-detection in critical conditions can lead to delayed treatment or worse outcomes for vulnerable populations.

Understanding the specific mode of fairness violation (unequal sensitivity vs. false positives) is crucial for developing targeted interventions and more responsible AI systems. This finding suggests that future work on multimodal fairness should prioritize methods that improve sensitivity across all subgroups.

## Strengths, Weaknesses, and Future Directions

### Strengths of CareBench

   

•

        **Comprehensive and Reproducible Benchmark:** CareBench stands out as the first systematic benchmark to simultaneously investigate multimodal EHR-CXR fusion across multiple dimensions: predictive performance, robustness to missingness, and algorithmic fairness. Its open-source nature, including the data extraction pipeline and model toolkit, ensures reproducibility and extensibility for the broader research community.

   

   

•

        **Rigorous Evaluation Protocol:** Moving beyond standard predictive accuracy, the benchmark's inclusion of detailed analyses for modality missingness and subgroup disparities provides a much-needed, clinically relevant perspective. This holistic approach offers deeper insights into model behavior under real-world conditions.

   

   

•

        **Actionable Clinical Insights:** The findings provide clear, actionable guidance on when multimodal learning helps, when it fails, and, crucially, _why_. Distinguishing between modality-distributed and modality-localized phenotypes, and identifying the impact of EHR's longitudinal richness, offers valuable clinical explanations for observed performance patterns.

   

   

•

        **Focus on Real-World Challenges:** By explicitly addressing pervasive issues like modality missingness and the critical need for fairness, CareBench tackles challenges that are often overlooked in academic benchmarks but are paramount for successful clinical deployment.

   

### Limitations of the Study

   

•

        **Specific Modality Focus:** While comprehensive for EHR and CXR, the benchmark is limited to these two modalities. Clinical practice often involves even more diverse data types, such as pathology reports, genetic data, or other imaging modalities (e.g., CT scans), which are not covered here.

   

   

•

        **Dataset Specificity:** The benchmark relies on MIMIC-IV and MIMIC-CXR datasets, which, while large and representative of a specific critical care setting (BIDMC), may not fully generalize to all healthcare systems or patient populations globally. Further validation on other multi-center datasets would strengthen generalizability.

   

   

•

        **Limited Fairness Attributes:** The fairness analysis focused solely on race. While crucial, other sensitive attributes like gender, socioeconomic status, or specific comorbidities could also introduce disparities and warrant investigation in future work.

   

   

•

        **Architectural Complexity vs. Clinical Nuance:** While CareBench explores a wide array of models, it highlights that even sophisticated architectures can struggle with modality imbalance and missingness if not explicitly designed for these challenges. This suggests that future model development needs to integrate clinical understanding more deeply into architectural design.

   

### Broader Impact and Future Directions

The CareBench benchmark provides a vital foundation for developing clinically deployable multimodal AI systems. The clear findings about the conditions under which multimodal fusion helps, the importance of specialized designs for missing data, and the critical need for explicit fairness considerations will significantly influence future research and development in clinical AI.

Future directions stemming from this work are manifold:

   

•

        **Developing Robust and Fair Architectures:** There is a clear need for new multimodal fusion models that intrinsically handle modality imbalance and missingness, perhaps through adaptive learning strategies or more sophisticated disentanglement mechanisms, while also being designed with fairness as a primary objective from the outset.

   

   

•

        **Expanding Modality Coverage:** Extending CareBench to include other critical clinical modalities, such as pathology reports, free-text clinical notes, or other imaging types, would further enhance its utility and provide a more complete picture of multimodal AI in healthcare.

   

   

•

        **Deeper Fairness Investigations:** Future work should explore fairness across a wider range of demographic and clinical subgroups, investigate different definitions of fairness, and develop methods to actively mitigate identified disparities, particularly focusing on improving sensitivity for under-detected groups.

   

   

•

        **Dynamic Multimodal Learning:** The richness of EHR data's temporal structure suggests a need for fusion models that can dynamically adapt to the varying information density and temporal dynamics of different modalities, rather than treating them as static inputs.

   

By releasing CareBench as an open-source toolkit, the authors have empowered the community to build upon their findings, accelerating the development of next-generation AI solutions that are not only powerful but also trustworthy and equitable, ultimately transforming patient care for the better.
